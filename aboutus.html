Shaggy

What is a Shaggy?

 Shaggy is a mysterious creature. It has been said that Shaggy often only uses 2% of its power. This wondrous being has been made out of Lego Technic bricks, Lego Mindstorm electronics, and no love; how depressing.

Tech Specs:
2x Light Sensors Delivers a spectacular black-and-white pixel intensity value, allowing Shaggy to navigate the grid.
2x Motors Performance motors, giving Shaggy barely adequate movement across the board.
1x Lego Mindstorm Brick The super-fast (for the 1990s) Linux-based processor. Allowing for blazingly mediocre computational speed for grid traversal.
1x Gyro Sensor The top-of-the-line in extremely temperamental gyroscopic technology. This instrument allows Shaggy to know its current rotation.

Shaggy Snapshots

Design Decisions:

 The first decision made was the most difficult: What mode of transportation will the robot have? Our eventual conclusion was that ground transportation was the most feasible, and as a result, we built it under the assumption that the robot would traverse on the board's surface. In addition, we concluded that the robot should have a compact design for the sake of a small footprint. This minimizes the potential causes of issues related to traversing the grid, especially lines.
 Since we only were given two motors and one roller, we made the robot front-wheel drive. This configuration gives us better control over the robot’s rotation. To further improve our ability to rotate the robot, we added a gyroscope. To keep the robot symmetrical and improve accuracy all while retaining compactness, we put the sensor in the center of the robot below its brain/Lego Mindstorm Brick.
 To detect grid lines, we added two light sensors, one on each side. This allows us to detect when we are crossing a line and if we need to rotate our robot. In addition, the position of the light sensor was important. We wanted the sensors as close to the wheels as possible. This is to minimize how much the "stationary" light sensor will travel when we rotate the robot, making the robot have to readjust less often.
 For the sake of compactness, multiple horned protrusions were added for wire management purposes.
